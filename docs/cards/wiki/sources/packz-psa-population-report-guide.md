# How to Use the PSA Population Report to Invest (Packz Blog)

## For future Claude
This page summarizes a Packz blog article (packz.io/blog/psa-population-report-guide) — covers the PSA Population Report as a daily-updated graded-card census, how grade distribution (especially PSA 10 rate) signals rarity, and the crack-and-resubmit inflation problem.
It feeds: [[concepts/population-report]], [[entities/psa]].
Key fact: the "PSA 10 rate below 5% = extreme grading difficulty and potential value premium" rule-of-thumb is tagged [Likely] — stated in this source without a PSA primary citation, and should not be presented to users as authoritative.

## TL;DR

- PSA Population Report is a daily-updated, publicly searchable database of every card PSA has ever graded, broken down by grade level.
- Grade distribution (how many PSA 10s specifically) matters more than total population for assessing rarity and value.
- Low pop ≠ guaranteed value; demand must accompany scarcity for a card to appreciate.

## Key claims

- The Population Report is "an online census for every trading card, ticket, pack, and pin that PSA has ever graded," searchable by player or set. `[Certain]`
- Data updated daily. `[Certain]`
- eBay integrates certification numbers with PSA's database, auto-populating listing grades for buyers. `[Certain]` — cited to sportscollectorsdigest.com within the source.
- "Population Higher" column shows how many cards have been graded above the searched grade. `[Certain]`
- PSA 10 rate below 10% = hard to grade; below 5% = extreme grading difficulty and potential value premiums. `[Likely]` — stated in this source without a PSA primary citation.
- Crack-and-resubmit can cause a single physical card to appear multiple times in the population count under different certification numbers. `[Certain]` — consistent across multiple sources.
- Vintage cards (1950s–60s) have lower populations due to smaller print runs and condition degradation over decades. `[Certain]`
- Junk wax era cards (late 1980s – early 1990s) were printed in massive quantities; high populations expected. `[Certain]`
- Submission rate matters as much as print run. A low pop can mean nobody submitted the card, not genuine rarity. `[Certain]` — corroborated by [[sources/psa-market-report-population-perspective]].
- For blue-chip cards (e.g., Jordan rookie, first-edition Charizard), demand is large enough that even high populations hold significant value. `[Certain]`
