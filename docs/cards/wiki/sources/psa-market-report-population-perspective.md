# Market Report: Population Perspective (PSA, Joe Orlando)

## For future Claude
This page summarizes a PSA primary-source article by Joe Orlando (psacard.com/articles/articleview/3631) — covers PSA's own definition of the Pop Report, the critical warning that low pop can reflect non-submission rather than rarity, and the fact that pop totals exclude rejected/altered cards and all unsubmitted raws.
It feeds: [[concepts/population-report]], [[entities/psa]].
Key fact: PSA directly warns in this article that a "Pop 4 Mint 9" card sold for ~$500 when its true value was ~$10 — this is the most concrete primary-source evidence for the submission-bias misreading risk, and should be cited in any product feature that surfaces pop data to users.

## TL;DR

- PSA's own article defines the Pop Report and warns directly that low pop can mean nobody submitted the card, not that it's rare — cites a "$500 for a $10 card" example.
- For pre-war and legitimately hard-to-find vintage cards, low populations are meaningful signals of genuine scarcity.
- Pop numbers exclude rejected/altered cards and all unsubmitted raw cards; vintage pop skews toward high grades because low-grade vintage rarely gets submitted.

## Key claims

- "The PSA Population Report is a tally of every single card we have ever graded and encapsulated. The Report breaks down the card by set, player and grade." `[Certain]` — PSA's own definition; primary source.
- Population numbers "need to be taken in context, just like any other fact or statistic." `[Certain]`
- For pre-war and legitimately scarce vintage cards, low populations are meaningful — the cards are "truly tough." `[Certain]`
- Wayne Gretzky 1979 O-Pee-Chee rookie: first PSA 10 graded only after 11 years of frequent submissions — legitimately scarce. `[Certain]`
- Counter-example (direct warning): a "Pop 4 Mint 9" card sold for ~$500 when its true value was ~$10 — the low pop reflected non-submission inactivity, not rarity. `[Certain]` — most concrete primary-source warning about pop misreading.
- "The population numbers do not represent ALL CARDS IN EXISTENCE, they merely represent the ones that have been graded by PSA." `[Certain]` — direct quote.
- Population numbers also exclude cards rejected for evidence of alteration, restoration, or questionable authenticity. `[Certain]` — detail not present in other sources.
- Most people don't submit low-grade vintage cards → pop skews toward high grades → if all vintage cards were graded, lower-grade population counts would be far larger. `[Certain]`
